# I Scanned My Fridge With AI and Got 3 Real Dinners — Here's What Actually Worked

Friday night, my fridge looked like everyone's fridge looks on a Friday night: half a rotisserie chicken, a bag of spinach starting to go slick, three eggs, half an onion wrapped in foil, a wedge of parmesan, and a jar of olives I forgot I bought. Nothing that obviously went together. So instead of defaulting to scrambled eggs again, I took a photo of the open fridge and ran it through an AI recipe scanner to see what it would actually suggest — and whether the suggestions would hold up once I was standing at the stove.

Three dinners came out of it. Two were genuinely good. One needed a fix mid-cook. Here's what worked, what didn't, and the technique details that made the difference, because "AI told me to" isn't a recipe — execution still matters.

## Dinner 1: Crispy Chicken & Spinach Skillet (the easy win)

This one used the rotisserie chicken, spinach, half the onion, and parmesan. The suggestion was basically a deconstructed gratin, and the technique that made it work was rendering flavor into the pan before adding anything green.

I sliced the onion thin and sweated it in olive oil over medium-low heat for about 8 minutes — not browned, just soft and sweet — then bumped the heat to medium-high, shredded in the chicken, and let it sit untouched for 2 minutes so the edges actually crisped instead of just heating through. Wilting the spinach is where people rush: add it in two batches, not all at once, so the pan temperature doesn't crash and the greens steam instead of sauté. Finish with parmesan off the heat so it melts into the residual moisture instead of clumping.

## Dinner 2: Olive & Egg Pan-Fry (the surprise)

I wouldn't have paired eggs with olives on my own, but the scanner suggested a Mediterranean-style skillet — basically a loose, savory egg scramble with chopped olives and the rest of the onion. The technique detail that mattered: salt the olives' brine flavor *into* the eggs early, not as a garnish. I whisked the eggs with a splash of the olive brine itself instead of plain salt, which seasoned them evenly instead of leaving pockets of saltiness. Low heat, constant folding (not stirring) with a spatula, pulled off the heat while still slightly underset — they finish cooking in the residual heat on the plate.

## Dinner 3: The one that needed a fix

The third suggestion tried to stretch the same ingredients into a "fried rice" using leftover rice I didn't actually have, just listed as "rice (if available)." That's the honest limit of any photo-based suggestion: it can see what's in your fridge, but not your pantry, and it will sometimes assume an ingredient that isn't there. I swapped in orzo, which needed a different cook time and more oil to keep it from gluing together — a fine save, but not a "just follow the recipe" win. Treat suggestions as a strong starting point, not a locked instruction set.

## What made the good suggestions actually useful

The two dinners that worked shared something: the AI grouped ingredients by what they'd taste like *together*, not just what happened to be in frame. That's a harder problem than it sounds — most "use up your leftovers" tools just list your ingredients back at you. ## FAQ

**Does an AI fridge scanner actually know what's good together, or just what's there?**
A decent one ranks by flavor compatibility, not just inventory — the chicken-spinach-parmesan pairing above is a real combination, not a random list. But it still can't taste-test for you, so treat the output as a strong draft.

**What if it suggests an ingredient I don't have, like the "rice if available" issue?**
Read suggestions as flexible, not literal. Swap in whatever you have with a similar role (a grain, a starch, a green) and adjust cook time accordingly — that's normal kitchen substitution, not a failure of the tool.

**Do I need good lighting or a tidy fridge for the photo to work?**
No tidying required — my test fridge had foil-wrapped odds and ends and a half-used jar in the door, and it still picked up the open items fine. A well-lit, in-focus shot of the shelf helps more than organization does.
